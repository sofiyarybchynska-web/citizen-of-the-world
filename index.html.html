01 — About Me

My Story

My name is Sofiya Rybchynska. I was born and raised in Kyiv, Ukraine, until the age of 14, when the full-scale Russian invasion on February 24th, 2022 forced me to leave behind my friends, family, city, and everything I had known. I relocated to Braunschweig, Germany, where I lived for three years before moving to Vancouver to study in the Faculty of Science at UBC, where I aspire to pursue the joint UBC-BCIT Biotechnology programme in my upper years.


I currently study on the unceded territories of the Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh First Nations. For a long time, I felt as though I was drowning — struggling to understand why the people I love had to endure such suffering, helpless watching my parents be separated across borders by martial law, and now facing my father's militarization. Yet through it all, I have tried to stay strong — for my parents, my family, my friends, and myself.

02 — Positionality Statement

I firmly believe that inaction is a form of complicity — every contribution, no matter how small, matters.

My mother has worked in education for high school and university students throughout her career, and currently leads seminars on the European Union in Ukraine. As early as 2014, she was involved in projects supporting Ukrainian refugees from the eastern regions where the conflict first began. Even as a child, she would bring me along to volunteer — I remember helping decorate celebratory baked goods for people who had lost their homes. Watching her became the foundation of my own sense of civic responsibility.

While living in Germany, I tutored mathematics online for children in Ukraine who lacked access to educational resources due to displacement. During summers when I was able to visit my father, I volunteered at a military hospital supporting wounded soldiers. In Germany, I also helped fellow students by providing simultaneous translation at academic conferences and assisting others during medical appointments. Beyond direct action, I consistently amplified news and awareness about Ukraine on social media, and participated in rallies and protests. 04 — My Reflection

Thinking Out Loud

What I learned from participating.

We Fight for Our Freedom
On February 22nd, my friend and I attended a rally marking the fourth anniversary of the full-scale invasion of Ukraine — a gathering held to raise awareness and demonstrate resilience against Russian aggression. I was moved by the sheer number of people who showed up. They were not only Ukrainians and Ukrainian-Canadians, but people from Iran, Hong Kong, Georgia, and beyond — united by a shared and deeply personal pain: the loss, or the fear of losing, a home.

Right now, across multiple countries, countless individuals are fighting for their lives and their freedom. It is nothing but luck that our greatest daily dilemma is deciding what to eat for dinner. It is sheer coincidence that we can say goodbye to a friend and know with certainty that we will see them next week — that we can leave a room, a school, a home, and trust that we will return to it. This is something we must never take for granted, because for people living in active conflict zones, tomorrow is never guaranteed. And because of that, it is our responsibility to support them in whatever way we are able — because this could just as easily have been us, had we simply been born somewhere else.

We unite in pain — that is what brought so many different faces and flags to that rally. But we must also unite in hope: hope for a future where people, and the generations that follow them, are free.
